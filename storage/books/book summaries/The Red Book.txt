The Core Thesis: C.G. Jung's *The Red Book: Liber Novus* (Reader's Edition), meticulously edited and introduced by Sonu Shamdasani, chronicles Jung's deeply personal and transformative "confrontation with the unconscious" from 1913 to 1930. The book argues that this intense self-experimentation, documented through active imagination, visionary experiences, and symbolic painting, served as the crucible for nearly all of his later psychological theories, fundamentally shaping his concepts of the collective unconscious, archetypes, individuation, and the integration of opposites, and representing his attempt to rediscover a living mythology for modern humanity amidst a cultural crisis.

Key Concepts & Definitions:

*   **Confrontation with the Unconscious:** Jung's deliberate and sustained period of intense self-exploration (1913-1930) where he intentionally engaged with his inner psychic processes, fantasies, and visionary experiences. It was a rigorous "self-experiment" to "get to the bottom of [his] inner processes."
*   **Active Imagination:** A psychological technique developed by Jung during his self-experimentation, involving consciously engaging with unconscious material (dreams, fantasies, visions) as if they were real, with a dialogical and interactive approach. It means deliberately evoking a fantasy in a waking state and then entering into it "as into a drama."
*   **Liber Novus (The Red Book):** Jung's personal, handwritten, and illustrated manuscript where he recorded the majority of his experiences, dialogues with inner figures, and reflections during his confrontation with the unconscious. He considered it the "nucleus" of his later works.
*   **Black Books:** Jung's private notebooks where he first recorded his fantasies, dreams, and inner states in dated entries, which later formed the basis for *Liber Novus*. They are seen as the "records of an experiment."
*   **Spirit of the Depths vs. Spirit of This Time:** A central dichotomy introduced by Jung. The "spirit of this time" represents the prevailing rational, scientific, and worldly consciousness, concerned with utility and convention. The "spirit of the depths" refers to the irrational, archaic, myth-making forces of the unconscious, which demand profound engagement and often challenge conscious values.
*   **Individuation:** The universal psychological process of developing one's unique personality. As presented in *Liber Novus*, it involves integrating conscious and unconscious elements, confronting inner figures, and balancing opposites to achieve psychological wholeness (the Self). *Liber Novus* represents the "prototype" of this process.
*   **The Self:** A central archetype and the totality of the psyche, encompassing both conscious and unconscious elements. It is the organizing center of the personal and collective unconscious and the goal of individuation, often represented by mandalas or paradoxical figures.
*   **Anima/Animus:** Archetypal figures representing the contralateral unconscious sexual component of the personality. For a man, the anima is the feminine aspect of his psyche; for a woman, the animus is the masculine aspect. Engaging with these figures is crucial for integration.
*   **Archetypes:** Primordial, universal patterns and images that derive from the collective unconscious, manifesting in myths, dreams, religions, and fantasies across cultures. They are "typical myths" or "primordial images."
*   **Pleroma:** A Gnostic concept, central to the *Septem Sermones ad Mortuos*, referring to "nothingness or fullness," the undifferentiated primordial ground from which creation and all opposites arise. It is both everything and nothing.
*   **Abraxas:** A Gnostic deity conceived by Jung in the *Septem Sermones* as a God encompassing and transcending both good and evil, unity and multiplicity. He is the "effectual itself," the "mother of good and evil," and a manifestation of the dynamic interplay within the Pleroma.
*   **God-image:** Jung distinguishes between the metaphysical concept of God and the psychological reality of the God-image, which refers to the inner, archetypal image of the divine as it is experienced in the human psyche. The God-image can evolve and be transformed through conscious engagement.
*   **Symbol:** For Jung, a living symbol (unlike a mere sign) is an image that points to something beyond conscious understanding and dynamically unifies conscious and unconscious contents. It is a "mediator" and "binds the rational and irrational."

**FRONT MATTER/PREFACE/INTRODUCTORY ESSAY**

*   **Preface to the Reader's Edition (Ulrich Hoerni):**
    *   Highlights the decade since *The Red Book*'s initial facsimile publication in 2009 and its overwhelming global success.
    *   Emphasizes the careful consideration that went into making the work accessible to a broad audience, respecting its multi-layered nature (psychological history, general readership, visual arts, calligraphy, collectors).
    *   States that the *Reader's Edition* aims to allow deep engagement with Jung's "inner development," aligning with Jung's intention for readers to make their reading fruitful for their own development.
*   **Preface (Ulrich Hoerni):**
    *   Traces the genesis of *The Red Book* from Jung's pivotal "confrontation with the unconscious" (1913-1930).
    *   Describes Jung's method of "active imagination" and how he recorded fantasies in his *Black Books*, then refined and calligraphically copied them into *Liber Novus* with his own paintings.
    *   Notes that Jung shared experiences with close associates but kept the *Red Book* private, leaving it unfinished despite considering publication.
    *   Explains Jung's reluctance to publish: it was unfinished, he diverted to alchemy, and he considered it autobiographical rather than scholarly.
    *   Details the Society of Heirs' initial decision to keep it private, its eventual photographic duplication for family use in 1984, and the later reevaluation in the 1990s as psychology history gained prominence.
    *   Explains Sonu Shamdasani’s role in developing a publication proposal, leading to the 2000 decision to release it, based on new understandings that Jung did not consider it fully secret and that it was central to his later work.
*   **Liber Novus: The “Red Book” of C.G. Jung by Sonu Shamdasani (Introduction):**
    *   **The Cultural Moment:** Places Jung's work within the early 20th-century artistic and psychological ferment, where artists and thinkers experimented with inner experience, blurring lines between literature, art, and psychology. It was a period of spiritual and cultural crisis, leading Jung to his "self-experimentation."
    *   **Who was C.G. Jung?:** Provides a biographical overview of Jung's early life, "first dream" (phallic, subterranean initiation), visual hallucinations, "terrible thought" (God's turd on the cathedral), and disillusionment with the Church.
    *   **The Two Personalities (No. 1 & No. 2):** Describes Jung's dual nature: the practical schoolboy (No. 1) and the solitary, religiously reflective individual connected to nature and history (No. 2). This duality influenced his career choice (medicine to reconcile science and humanities).
    *   **Early Influences & Psychiatry:** Details Jung's interest in Nietzsche, Schopenhauer, Swedenborg, and spiritualism, especially his séances with Helene Preiswerk. His medical dissertation analyzed spiritualistic phenomena psychologically (influenced by Flournoy and James). His Burghölzli work on associations and complexes established his reputation.
    *   **Jung and Freud:** Clarifies that Jung's work was not solely derived from Freud, emphasizing his independent roots in Bleuler, Janet, and Flournoy. They collaborated to establish scientific psychotherapy based on new psychology.
    *   **The Intoxication of Mythology:** Describes Jung's shift to mythology, folklore, and religion culminating in *Transformations and Symbols of the Libido* (1911-1912). This work distinguished "directed thinking" from "fantasy thinking" and interpreted myths (especially the hero myth) as symbols of libido, foreshadowing the collective unconscious. His realization of his "mythlessness" spurred his self-experiment.
    *   **"My Most Difficult Experiment":** Discusses Jung's significant dreams in 1912 (Austrian customs guard/knight, gull/child) that challenged Freudian theory and led him to seek deeper meaning. He began treating fantasies as real, akin to artistic creation, but encountered resistance from a "voice" he identified as his anima.
    *   **The Visions of 1913-1914:** Details Jung's precognitive visions of a devastating flood and a blood-red glow over northern lands, which he initially feared indicated his own madness but later understood as the impending collective catastrophe of World War I. This realization validated his inner experiences and prompted him to record them in *Liber Novus*.
    *   **Liber Novus (Composition):** Explains that *Liber Novus* transcribed *Black Book* fantasies, adding extensive lyrical elaborations and commentaries. Its structure and style were influenced by Nietzsche’s *Zarathustra* and Dante's *Commedia*, depicting a descent into a personal Hell to shape an individual cosmology. It was intended for a public, embodying Jung's new "constructive method" of interpretation.
        *   **Art and the Zürich School:** Discusses Jung's artistic explorations (painting, mandalas) and their connection to broader artistic experimentation within his circle (Dadaists, Maeder, Moltzer, Riklin) during WWI. Jung saw his pictorial work as a form of "active imagination" but resisted calling it "art," prioritizing meaning and signification.
    *   **The Collective Experiment / The Return of the Dead:** Jung encouraged patients to engage in active imagination, seeing his own experiences as paradigmatic. Describes the parapsychological events in his house in 1916, leading to the writing of *Septem Sermones ad Mortuos* – a Gnostic creation myth culminating in the birth of Abraxas, symbolizing a transformation of the Western God-image.
    *   **The Mandalas:** Introduces Jung's drawing of mandalas (starting in 1916) as "cryptograms on the state of my self," revealing an unconscious developmental process and symbolizing psychological wholeness and the goal of individuation.
    *   **Scrutinies:** Describes this later manuscript as forming *Liber Tertius*, extending *Liber Novus* by transcribing fantasies from 1913-1916, interspersed with interpretive commentaries. It further clarifies the role of Philemon and the "reborn God."
    *   **The Content:** *Liber Novus* explores self-knowledge, the soul, psychological types, integration of opposites, solitude, war, madness, the death of God, and the future of Western religion. Its overarching theme is regaining the soul and developing a new psychological and theological cosmology—the prototype of individuation.
    *   **"A New Spring of Life" (Post-1916 Development):** Jung began translating *Liber Novus*'s themes into psychological language, outlining his mature psychology: personal vs. impersonal unconscious, persona, anima, individuation as higher development. He emphasized "active imagination" as a therapeutic technique for both himself and his patients.
    *   **The Way to the Self (1918-1921):** Jung posited the symbol as unifying rational and irrational truth. His paper on "The psychological foundations of the belief in spirits" linked personal and collective unconscious activation, viewing his own WWI visions as collective precognition. He formalized psychological types and introduced the "Self" as a psychological concept, incorporating the anima/animus.
    *   **Publication Deliberations:** Details Jung's extensive discussions with colleagues (Cary Baynes, Wolfgang Stockmayer) about publishing *Liber Novus*, revealing his apprehension about it being misunderstood as madness, despite its centrality to his work. Cary Baynes's notes record her attempts to persuade him of its universal significance.
    *   **The Transformation of Psychotherapy:** Jung's experience led to a new model of psychotherapy, not just for treating pathology, but for fostering individual development and individuation through active imagination. He encouraged patients to create their own "Red Books."
    *   **Philemon's Sanctuary:** Describes Jung's retreat to Bollingen and the construction of his tower (starting 1920) as a 3D continuation of *Liber Novus*, a "confession in stone" and representation of individuation. His mother's death in 1923 and the Liverpool dream (1927) marked significant turns in his understanding of the anima and the Self as the goal of individuation.
    *   **The Confrontation with the World:** Jung ceased direct work on *Liber Novus* in 1930, redirecting his energy to alchemy after receiving *The Secret of the Golden Flower* from Richard Wilhelm. This text provided external confirmation of his internal experiences, allowing him to externalize his inner work.
    *   **The Comparative Study of the Individuation Process:** After 1930, Jung used alchemy and other esoteric practices as parallels to his individuation process, transforming his subjective experiences into a comparative scientific psychology presented in his *Collected Works*, which amplified and indirectly commented on *Liber Novus*.
    *   **The Publication of Liber Novus:** Recounts the family’s guardianship of the manuscript after Jung's death, his later authorization for Aniela Jaffé to cite from it (though she largely didn't), and the eventual publication thanks to the efforts of Sonu Shamdasani and the Jung heirs. Emphasizes that the publication "marks a caesura" in the understanding of Jung's work.
*   **Translators’ Note (Mark Kyburz, John Peck, and Sonu Shamdasani):**
    *   Addresses the linguistic challenges of translating *Liber Novus*'s three stylistic registers: reportorial (fantasies/dialogues), conceptual (reflections), and mantic/prophetic.
    *   Explains that these registers often rub against each other, creating a "polyphonic mode."
    *   Details the lack of prior models for translation and the decision to maintain Jung's original exploratory, somewhat "shaggy" style, rather than over-modernizing or archaizing.
    *   Notes that Jung's language underwent a "descent into hell" to renew the capacity for utterance, and the translation aims to convey this "compositional texture intact."
    *   Mentions specific translation choices, like using the King James Version for biblical tonalities and freshly rendering *Collected Works* citations to align with the *Liber Novus* context.
    *   Highlights Jung's "complex respect for language" and the "destabilizing experience" of his linguistic journey, culminating in his confident consignment of the book to its "depth trajectory" despite its unfinished state.
*   **Editorial Note (Sonu Shamdasani):**
    *   Explains that *Liber Novus* is an unfinished corpus with no single final version, necessitating editorial decisions regarding presentation.
    *   Outlines the sequence of extant manuscripts (*Black Books*, handwritten/typed drafts, calligraphic volume, Cary Baynes's transcription).
    *   Describes the "second layer" of the text (Jung's commentaries) as crucial to understanding his "attempt at an elaboration."
    *   Details how significant changes between versions are noted in footnotes to illustrate Jung's continuous revision.
    *   Explains the inclusion of *Scrutinies* as *Liber Tertius* due to strong narrative and thematic continuities with *Liber Primus* and *Liber Secundus*.
    *   Discusses formatting choices: paragraphing based on Cary Baynes's transcription and Jung's use of colored initials/bolding.
*   **Note to the Reader's Edition:** States that this edition reproduces the complete text, introduction, and notes of the original, laid out in one column, and updates references to Jung's 1925 seminar.

**LIBER PRIMUS**

*   **Prologue: The Way of What Is to Come (with Isaiah and John citations):**
    *   Jung asserts his compulsion to speak, not from the "spirit of this time" (utility, value) but from the "spirit of the depths" (irrational depths of modernism).
    *   The spirit of the depths stripped him of conventional scientific belief, forcing him to embrace the "inexplicable and paradoxical" – a "melting together of sense and nonsense" that produces "supreme meaning."
    *   The "supreme meaning" is the path to "the God yet to come," a paradoxical image with both light and shadow, simultaneously great and small, cosmic and cellular.
    *   He recounts his visions of a devastating flood and a blood-red sea just before WWI, which he initially feared as personal madness but later understood as collective, precognitive events, indicating the "spirit of the depths" was also the "ruler of the depths of world affairs." These visions validated his inner experiences.
    *   He declares that his offering is not a teaching but "news of the way of this man," urging readers to find their *own* way, truth, and life within themselves, advocating against living by example or seeking external laws. "The way is within us, but not in Gods, nor in teachings, nor in laws."
*   **Chapter I: Refinding the Soul:**
    *   Jung describes a personal crisis in his fortieth year (1913), having achieved worldly success, yet feeling a void and horror. He realized he had "lost [his] soul."
    *   The spirit of the depths compelled him to speak to his soul as a "living and self-existing being," affirming that his judgment and knowledge were objects *of his soul*, not vice-versa.
    *   Highlights that detaching desire from external things leads to the "place of the soul." Failure to do so results in endless, desperate pursuit of "hollow things of the world."
    *   Emphasizes the soul's wealth residing in "images" and the importance of nourishing the soul to avoid breeding "dragons and devils."
*   **Chapter II: Soul and God:**
    *   Jung addresses his soul, acknowledging a long separation and his rediscovery of her "behind all" events, first in "images within men and then you yourself."
    *   He grapples with the paradoxical nature of his soul, revealed in dreams as both child and maiden, questioning if "God is a child, a maiden?"
    *   He recounts the soul's guidance, taking away when he sought to take hold, giving when he expected nothing, and revealing fate from unexpected quarters.
    *   Stresses that dreams are "guiding words of the soul"; the "spirit of the depths" transcends conventional notions of beauty, cleverness, and foolishness.
    *   Introduces the "knowledge of the heart," attainable only by fully living one's unlived life, including both good and evil.
    *   The soul asserts Jung's role as a "servant of a child," promoting extreme humility. His "God in my soul is a child."
    *   Paradoxically states: "If you are boys, your God is a woman. If you are women, your God is a boy. If you are men, your God is a maiden." The God is "where you are not," continuously coming into being.
    *   Urges becoming "Christs" not "Christians," overcoming old teachings by doing "more," emphasizing individuation rather than imitation.
*   **Chapter III: On the Service of the Soul:**
    *   Jung expresses torment and doubt over the demand to follow his soul into meaninglessness or madness, lamenting his intellectual pride.
    *   The soul assures him that "disorder and meaninglessness are the mother of order and meaning," and that marrying chaos and order produces the "divine child."
    *   He confronts his fear of the depths, acknowledging that "love is also terrible," and must embrace even what horrifies him, like the saint drinking pus that smelled of roses.
    *   He must become a servant of his soul, for "if you are the master of your soul, then become her servant. If you were her servant, make yourself her master."
    *   The spirit of the depths commands him to "Look into your depths, pray to your depths, waken the dead."
*   **Chapter IV: The Desert / Experiences in the Desert:**
    *   Jung's soul leads him into the "desert of my own self," a desolate place where his conscious thoughts are powerless, and he must "wait."
    *   He realizes his personal "self became a desert" due to living too much outside himself through external things, people, and thoughts.
    *   Emphasizes that "no culture of the mind is enough to make a garden out of your soul," but turning creative force inward makes the soul "green."
    *   Recalls the ancients going into the desert to find their souls, symbolizing the soul's lonely place and the abundance of visions found there.
    *   The desert teaches "terrible simplemindedness" and "poverty of spirit," which cleverness scorns. Overcoming scorn makes the desert green.
*   **Chapter V: Descent into Hell in the Future:**
    *   Jung experiences a terrifying vision of descending into a dark cave, encountering a bloody human head, a black scarab, countless serpents, and a "red sun of the depths."
    *   He struggles to reconcile this with reason and science, realizing the "divine madness" of the soul's world and the inadequacy of intellect to grasp it.
    *   Distinguishes between "sick" and "divine delusion," explaining that genuine connection with the spirit of the depths (divine madness) is frightening but necessary, while sick delusion arises from an imbalance.
    *   Interprets the vision as precognitive of the "murder" (WWI), stating that "in the depths of what is to come lay murder." The black beetle symbolizes necessary death for renewal.
    *   Emphasizes that "life does not come from events, but from us," and "we create the meaning of events" for our individual "way of salvation."
    *   Acknowledges his participation in the "murder" (war), identifying as both "murderer and murdered, sacrificer and sacrificed," seeing the war as a collective externalization of an internal conflict.
    *   The war is an atonement, accelerating humanity's "ripening" by forcing the "sacrifice of the hero in themselves" rather than killing their brothers.
*   **Chapter VI: Splitting of the Spirit:**
    *   Jung's descent into "Hell" feels like becoming Hell itself, surrounded by invisible, "magical" beings that change his form into a "monstrous animal."
    *   The spirit of the depths commands him to "Sit yourself down, be calm," despite his anger and bewilderment at the "nonsense" of his inner experiences.
    *   A dialogue with his soul reveals the radical difference in perspective: what he calls "darkness," the soul calls "light," which is "not of this world."
    *   The soul claims his words and thoughts originate from her, leading to a realization of "civil war" within him: "my indignation must also come from you, and in me you are indignant against yourself."
    *   He sees himself transformed into a "rapacious beast," carrying the "murder" and "war" within, foreseeing external events as internal processes ("civil war in me").
    *   "Everything that becomes too old becomes evil... If a God ceases being the way of life, he must fall secretly." The "spirit of the depths" takes over when the "spirit of this time" has peaked.
*   **Chapter VII: Murder of the Hero:**
    *   Jung dreams of murdering Siegfried, the blond German hero, with a youth. This symbolizes the necessary killing of the "hero ideal of my efficiency" and the "God" of the old year for renewal.
    *   The statement "The highest truth is one and the same with the absurd" serves as a saving insight, releasing him from psychological tension.
    *   In the aftermath, he sees a "merry garden" with white-clad figures in colored light, symbolizing his rebirth.
    *   He reflects that Gods need to be overcome ("killed") to be renewed, and humanity kills its princes when it cannot kill its Gods in themselves. The devil ("shadow, nonsense") emerges when a God grows old.
    *   The "blood of my hand" falling as "rain" symbolizes the "great stream of tears that will come over the peoples" (WWI), leading to mourning, rebirth, and the "fructifying of the earth" for a "new wheat, the young, germinating God."
*   **Chapter VIII: The Conception of the God:**
    *   Jung finds the new world "weak and artificial," but the spirit of the depths reveals it as the "mustard seed" of a new God, a "fruit that is older than the tree."
    *   The divine child is born from "terrible ambiguity" ("hateful-beautiful, evil-good, laughable-serious, sick-healthy, inhuman-human and ungodly-godly").
    *   The new God is found in the "relative," embracing the fullness of life, rather than the absolute good/beautiful. Christ's descent into Hell to become his Antichrist is a parallel.
    *   The depths are more powerful than humanity, urging caution: "He who journeys to Hell also becomes Hell." This involves accepting "evil," but with "golden cups full of the sweet drink of life" to enliven dead matter, transforming it into "black serpents"—a process leading to a "new sun" and "night with wonderful will-o'-the-wisps."
    *   Hell is defined as confronting "all that you no longer are or are not yet capable of," realizing the "laughable" and "brutal" in the fine and good. The deepest Hell is realizing Hell isn't just Hell but a "cheerful Heaven"—the "ambiguity of the God."
    *   The new God arises from the "melting together" of opposites (mockery/worship, grief/laughter, yes/no), born from his soul like a virgin conceiving. He is a "new God" who laughs at imitation and forces "men through himself."
    *   The world appears cold and empty as the old "hero" (imitation) falls. Solitude with the self enables the new God to move into this emptiness, leading to connection with the "God of others" and the "true neighbor."
*   **Chapter IX: Mysterium. Encounter:**
    *   Jung encounters Elijah (spirit/Logos, vision, foresight) and his blind daughter Salome (feeling/Eros, temptation, dark allure) in a dark, subterranean hall. A black serpent also accompanies them.
    *   He is shocked by Salome's connection to the prophet, recognizing the "most extreme contradiction" ("unholy woman" and "mouth of God").
    *   Salome tempts him to embrace her "devilish" nature, implying that "a sin" might be worth a "single look into the infinite unfolding of what is to come."
    *   She reveals her paradoxical nature: "I am your sister," and Mary is their mother. This challenges his Christian conceptions and plunges him into "terrible confusion."
    *   The black serpent, winding up a tree, symbolizes the "earthly essence of man," separating Logos and Eros, but also connecting them through "longing" – a "wise bridge."
    *   Jung realizes his need to balance thinking (Prometheus) and pleasure (blind, powerful Eros), overcoming their separation and accepting their "intimate community." If he, a thinker, loves Salome, it means embracing his neglected feeling function.
*   **Chapter X: Instruction:**
    *   Jung re-enters the subterranean realm, still confused. Elijah and Salome present varying visions (Mother of God with child, Peter with key, Pope, Buddha, Kali) reflecting his inner turmoil and the multifaceted nature of the divine.
    *   Elijah confronts his "ignorance" and "bad conscience," explaining that his thoughts are "outside [his] self," like trees and animals, leading to "psychological objectivity."
    *   Salome reveals Mary as their mother, implying Jung is Christ and she is his sister. He is horrified but eventually embraces the idea that this is a "mystery of the transformation" where "forethinking and pleasure unite" to produce "the divine son, who is the supreme meaning."
    *   He becomes a "servant" of this transformation, like Peter locking the past and opening the future. He sees himself as a "mother to the God," despite his disdain for the "cruel Goddess of my interior, my womanly pleasure, my own other."
    *   He acknowledges the paradox: his "thinking is the son of forethinking," and his "pleasure is the daughter of love."
*   **Chapter XI: Resolution:**
    *   Jung witnesses a battle between a black and a white serpent on a mountain ridge, representing the struggle between good and evil, dark and light. The black serpent's head turns white, signifying a triumph of good.
    *   Elijah takes him to a "temple of the sun" on the summit, where Mime (a dwarf) explains that the "collected light becomes water" and "flows in many springs." This signifies gaining wisdom from the depths.
    *   He then witnesses the serpent coiling around the cross, entering the crucified body, and emerging white from its mouth, crowning the dead one's head like a diadem, with the sun rising.
    *   His white bird (soul) encourages: "Let it rain, let the wind blow, let the waters flow and the fire burn. Let each thing have its development, let becoming have its day."
    *   Salome appears, stating he is Christ and Mary is their mother, reinforcing the idea of his "imitation of the Lord in his final torment." The serpent wraps around his body, and his face becomes that of a lion (Deus Leontocephalus).
    *   He is "crucified" yet "triumphant," his task now fulfilled, and Elijah tells him to "Seek untiringly, and above all write exactly what you see."
    *   Jung reflects that this vision meant he must "remain true to love" to resolve inner conflicts, accepting the "dismembering" that leads to liberation. The crown and serpent are "opposites," and yet "one," signifying the mysterious unity of love.
    *   The sun sinks into the sea, and he glides with it, while the bird (serpent transformed) reminds him: "If you love the earth, you are hanged; if you love the sky, you hover." He learns the sun and earth are one, and the crown means love.
    *   He enters a period of quiet, internal work, realizing he must build his inner "tower" as a secure refuge, with the "devil forged into the foundations." His *I* becomes a "barbarian" that must be educated and purified.

**LIBER SECUNDUS**

*   **Cap. I: The Red One:**
    *   Jung, feeling paralyzed after *Liber Primus*, encounters the "Red One" (initially appearing as the devil) on his castle tower. The Red One, a "red horseman" with "fiery red" clothing, questions Jung's seriousness and Christian devotion.
    *   They debate the nature of paganism vs. Christianity, the possibility of a "good Jew," and the validity of scientific vs. ethical truths.
    *   The Red One morphs, revealing himself as "joy"—the "joy of the serious person," which encompasses passion, wildness, and unpredictability, but also leads to temptations.
    *   Jung realizes he must confront his "devil" (his own joy) with seriousness, as it represents his "other standpoint" and is a necessary element for understanding. He embraces this "devil" (joy), which makes him metaphorically "green like a tree in spring."
    *   He learns that joy, like fire, can destroy and that taking it seriously involves accepting its "evil" aspects, leading to "life and its disappointment, from which the wholeness of our life becomes."
*   **Cap. II: The Castle in the Forest:**
    *   Jung gets lost in a forest and finds an old castle where a reclusive scholar, absorbed in books, lives with his pale, "bewitched" daughter. This daughter embodies the "romantic"—a "banal", "fairy-tale-like" figure initially despised but then revealed as a "real" and "beautiful" soul yearning for redemption.
    *   He fights his "impotent" and "childish" thoughts of a romantic heroine, realizing the "banal" is as sacred and real as the "fabulous."
    *   He recognizes the universal human need for redemption, stating, "Only what is human and what you call banal and hackneyed contains the wisdom that you seek."
    *   He is challenged to accept his own femininity (for a man), and masculinity (for a woman), to achieve "completeness" and true humanity, becoming "ridiculous" in the process but gaining freedom from societal or gender straitjackets.
    *   He realizes his "soul is in great need" like the scholar's daughter, suffering from the "drought" of an overly externalized, intellectual life. He must "make [his] ordered world horrible" to reconnect with the "wonder-world of his soul."
    *   His personal "Hell" is comprised of all the "odious and disgusting" things he has rejected, but by embracing them, he gives "soul to the soulless."
*   **Cap. III: One of the Lowly:**
    *   Jung encounters a "destitute" and "dirty" tramp with one eye (a cyclops-like figure) in a snow-covered landscape. This "lowly" man, a former convict, represents the "stream of heroic story" but also the "animal" in man, and film (cinema) as a modern myth-making medium.
    *   The tramp's casual acceptance of his criminal past and lack of conventional morality challenges Jung's intellectual and moral frameworks. He embodies a raw, unrefined form of human experience.
    *   The tramp's eventual death from a hidden illness (blood from the mouth) reveals the "terrible deceit of life" and the inevitability of death, emphasizing that "outer dying is better than inner death."
    *   Jung embraces this "destitute" figure, recognizing that without the "depths" (the lowest, common aspects of humanity), he cannot truly know the "heights." He must descend to the "holy stream of common life," becoming "a fish among fish, a frog among frogs."
    *   He differentiates between "being" (in the depths, endless growth) and "becoming" (on the heights, torment), needing both for renewal.
    *   He experiences himself as a "drop in the ocean," subject to vast, impersonal currents, realizing he needs to embrace collective death and "live within" while accepting "dying outside."
*   **Cap. IV: The Anchorite. Dies I [Day 1]:**
    *   Jung enters a hot, desolate desert and follows barefoot tracks to a hut, finding an anchorite (Ammonius) reading a Greek Gospel.
    *   Ammonius, a Christian hermit and former rhetorician from Alexandria, teaches Jung about the "multifaceted meaning" of words and the inadequacy of a single, literal interpretation, criticizing Philo's abstract Logos for usurping life.
    *   Ammonius asserts that John the Evangelist brought the Logos *down to man* (into the human realm), not elevated man to it.
    *   Jung discovers the deep stillness of the desert as essential for the solitary, allowing him to focus on the "sprouting garden of his soul" under the "immense sun." He re-experiences the anima of seemingly inanimate objects (stones, scarab).
    *   The anchorite's life, though outwardly monotonous, is intensely rich internally, nurturing a profound inner life ("delicious red fruit").
*   **Cap. V: Dies II [Day 2]:**
    *   Jung wakes up feeling a "primordially ancient dream" of the rising sun (Helios) with four white, winged horses, recognizing it as a pagan prayer.
    *   He grapples with his internal paganism (worshiping the sun, scarab, earth) and the contradiction with Christian faith, acknowledging that "one can never escape the age-old dreams of mankind."
    *   Ammonius clarifies that the Logos (revealed through Christ) is meaningful only when understood in human terms, not as an abstract concept. He explains that early conversion to Christianity involved understanding its teachings in terms of existing pagan belief systems.
    *   Ammonius reveals Christianity as a transformation of old Egyptian teachings, suggesting that "Every subsequent form of religion is the meaning of the antecedent." He emphasizes searching for the "meaning which is yet to come" in the Gospels.
    *   Ammonius, recognizing Jung's pagan leanings, suddenly accuses him of being "Satan," revealing the intense internal conflict and the danger of integrating such profound opposites. Jung then feels himself embracing his darkness, becoming a "greening tree."
    *   Jung reflects on the "magnificent night" of comprehending his darkness, leading to his "phoenix" rising from the depths, but simultaneously leading to the "terrible war" as external manifestation of internal conflicts.
    *   He realizes that "evil" is not just a general human vice but "stands precisely right behind your virtues, that it is also your virtues themselves."
    *   Embracing death is vital for finding joy in "smallest things" and for "ripening" life, as "dying increases life." The "inner death" is preferable to the "outer dying."
*   **Cap. VI: Death:**
    *   Jung journeys to the "northern land," a landscape of "cold death"—swamps, empty spaces, solitude—where he meets "Death" personified as a gaunt figure.
    *   He witnesses a "dense multitude of men, old men, women, and children," horses, animals, a "dead summer" being swept into a "sea of blood" and dissolving into "murky clouds of mist" (WWI).
    *   From this horror, a "new sun," bloody and burning, emerges from the sea of blood. This new sun is "awful" and a "reversal of that which we call day," making the darkness shine.
    *   Jung concludes that "today's men need a large slice of death" to balance the "incorrectness" in them. Accepting death "greens" life and allows harvesting of its fruit.
    *   The experience makes him realize that his "individual being" comes from encountering collective death, leading to a "solitary clear light of the night"—the "individual being" as a "near fragment of eternity."
    *   The scene becomes grotesque: jungle-like laughter, birth horrors, and the realization that "divine madness" is the "overpowering of the spirit of this time through the spirit of the depths."
*   **Cap. VII: The Remains of Earlier Temples:**
    *   Jung finds himself in a flowery meadow and encounters Ammonius (now a paunchy monk) and the Red One (now an old, shabby ascetic). They are repulsed by him, as he is "fully covered in green leaves."
    *   Ammonius blames Jung for "seducing" him into forming a monastery (away from true solitude), while the Red One blames him for making him serious. Both ultimately succumbed to life's temptations and "natural burial ground of all outlived ideals."
    *   Jung had absorbed their lives: a "green tree" has grown from the "ruins of the temple." He realizes he is no longer divided by their separate ways but has become a "forest goblin and prankster," merrily "greening and blooming" from within himself.
    *   He sees them as "deserted bridges" to mankind, unable to reconcile their "common misery." He realizes he must live as a complete man despite the tension, accepting his own "chameleonesque skin" and "solar nature" while shedding false ideals.
    *   He declares his path is solitary, beyond men's expectations or demands, "filling [his] solitariness with [his] life," becoming his own "noise, conversation, comfort, and help." He determines to go to the "far East" toward his "rising."
*   **Cap. VIII: First Day:**
    *   Jung's path takes him through a narrow mountain gorge, over black "hot iron" and white "ice," leading him to Izdubar (an early form of Gilgamesh), a bull-horned giant.
    *   Izdubar, the "mighty," is paralyzed by Jung's "poison" (scientific knowledge from the West, denying immortality and boundlessness). He laments his mortality and the loss of his power.
    *   Jung realizes he has wounded the giant by speaking "truth" (science), turning the hero into a sobbing child. He discerns a "magic word that counteracts the poison" – recognizing that truth can be poison.
    *   He builds a fire, introducing Izdubar to modern "magic" (matches, flying machines, timepieces), revealing the "advantages" of science and technological power but also its paralytic effect on belief.
    *   Izdubar, yearning for the "pure source of life-giving wisdom" of the East, reveals his blind, excessive longing and tempestuous force; Jung represents "seeing limitation and the incapacity of cleverness." They must unite their forces.
    *   The segment ends with Jung's compassionate decision not to let Izdubar die, recognizing him as his "brother" and his own "lot." He realizes that his own healing is bound up with healing his God.
*   **Cap. IX: Second Day:**
    *   Jung ponders how to help the paralyzed Izdubar. He realizes Izdubar is a "fantasy," not real in an ordinary sense, but real as a fantasy. This "unreal" nature makes him "light" enough to carry.
    *   He carries Izdubar (now light as a feather) through the mountains, seeking the houses of "hospitable men" to restore him. He avoids "enlightened" modern men who are "paralyzed" by "poisonous vapor" (scientific rationalism).
    *   He shrinks Izdubar to the size of an egg and puts him in his pocket, then enters a welcoming house where Izdubar can heal.
    *   Jung reflects that this process of declaring the God a "figment of the imagination" actually saved him, allowing him to be carried as a "living fantasy."
    *   He reinterprets the Christ figure: St. Christopher carries the Christ child; Jung, a "child," carries the giant. "Our yoke is sweet, and our burden is light" applies to inner fantasy, not external (unbearable) reality.
    *   He realizes that those who cling to outer, visible Gods are "blinded" and become "dark men," while those who embrace the inner God (fantasy) gain "seeing eyes."
    *   He embraces the "God whom you bear," but not his weapons (conquering). He wants to live in his "dark land" among unsuspecting people, and secretly carries his God to a "hospitable roof," where he can incubate it.
*   **Cap. X: The Incantations:**
    *   Jung performs incantations, preparing a "red rug" for his "God in the egg," identifying himself as the "mother," "simple maiden," "careful father," "shepherd," "holy animal," and "wise man from the East."
    *   The "God in the egg" is "eternal emptiness and eternal fullness," "double nature in one," with meaning in absurdity and freedom in bondage. It is the "light of the middle way," embryonic and full of ardor, awaiting lost memories.
    *   He sings praises to this God as the "lord of the beginning," "star of the East," and "beginning and the end."
    *   He recounts the sacrifices he has made for this God: slaying human sacrifice, cutting his skin, banishing his parents, overthrowing Gods, breaking laws, playing like a child in the sand, destroying his armor, exchanging goals.
    *   He admits he is "not ready," struggling with the "pitiableness of the God" (being in an egg), feeling the "demise of the Gods" as he holds it in his hand. This God cannot even be blasphemed against.
    *   He loves this "defenseless and hopeless one," recognizing that "We need your light, Oh child. Since we go in darkness, light up our paths." He becomes its "mother," nourishing it.
*   **Cap. XI: The Opening of the Egg:**
    *   Jung opens the egg, and Izdubar (the God) rises, enormous, transformed, proclaiming himself "completely sun," healed and radiant, having been "reborn."
    *   Jung, overwhelmed, falls on his face, feeling his own life force drain away as the God ascends. He becomes the "night's prey," mauled and bleeding, while the God takes his "life with him."
    *   "Woe betide the mother who gives birth to a God!" he exclaims, realizing the "hellish afterbirth" of all the monstrous serpents and dragons of eternal emptiness that follow a perfect God.
    *   He confronts the "chaos" of creation, realizing that "emptiness" (evil) now seeks to devour "fullness" (good). He recognizes the necessity of evil to dissolve old formations and regain freedom.
    *   He explains that "nature is playful and terrible," and both aspects must be embraced for new life to develop.
*   **Cap. XII: Hell:**
    *   Jung has a vision of a "gloomy vault" in Hell, where a maiden with red-gold hair (his soul) has stabbed a "man of devilish appearance" (the evil one) in the eye with a silver fishing rod.
    *   A voice declares: "The evil one cannot make a sacrifice... victory is with the one who can sacrifice."
    *   Jung realizes his soul has fallen into "abysmal evil," and to create his God (eternal fullness), he must also create "eternal emptiness" (evil). To escape evil is to create "tepid and gray" Gods.
    *   He embraces evil for the sake of his God, understanding that "nothing is more valuable to the evil one than his eye," as it seeks to drink in beauty. He must accept the "devilishness of human nature" and his own "shadow."
    *   He reflects on the "infinite gulf" separating humans and the "bridge that could connect us," noting the destructive potential of "unsuspected animality."
    *   His soul's "fishing rod" is her "will," which can strike the evil one's vulnerable spot. "He who does not want evil will have no chance to save his soul from Hell."
*   **Cap. XIII: The Sacrificial Murder:**
    *   Jung witnesses a gruesome scene in a desolate valley: a small girl's body, terribly wounded, head smashed, amidst stones. A veiled figure (the soul of the child) instructs him to "remove the liver" and "eat it."
    *   Horrified by this "desecration, necrophilia," he resists but is driven to perform the "horrific and absurd deed," for "you are a man, and a man has committed this deed."
    *   Eating the liver (the "sacrificial flesh") is an act of "atonement," a recognition of his complicity in human evil. The veiled figure reveals herself as his own soul, confirming that the "divine child" (God's formation) has been slain, and he must eat of its flesh to regenerate.
    *   He learns that "primordial powers" are added to his soul through this "sacrificial murder," healing it. "There are not many truths, there are only a few. Their meaning is too deep to grasp other than in symbols."
    *   The "primordial force" (radiance of the sun) now burns within him like an "inextinguishable fire," a "holy affliction" that compels him relentlessly. This makes him reject mundane "rationality" in favor of the "living wonders of the God."
    *   He resolves to serve his "self" by freeing it from the God's overpowering embrace, embracing the God's hate, powerlessness, and meaninglessness as well as love, beauty, and wisdom.
*   **Cap. XIV: Divine Folly:**
    *   Jung enters a library and encounters a librarian-philosopher, seeking Thomas à Kempis's *Imitation of Christ*. He debates the efficacy of Christian resignation vs. Nietzsche's "agitated" truth, noting that "truth unfortunately adheres more to the middle way."
    *   He realizes that "imitating Christ" means living one's *own* life, not following an external model ("break the Christ in yourself so that you may arrive at yourself").
    *   He confronts the librarian's "scholarly conceit" and the limitations of purely intellectual knowledge, preferring to seek "depressive truth" for those "forced into a corner."
    *   He accepts his "divine folly"—the unpredictable, paradoxical nature of his path.
*   **Cap. XV: Nox secunda [Second Night]:**
    *   In a kitchen, Jung encounters a cook and the ghost of Ezechiel, an Anabaptist prophet, leading a throng of "dead" who seek peace in Jerusalem but "forgot something important that should also have been lived."
    *   They accuse him ("dissonant daimon") of not living his "animal" nature.
    *   He is arrested by policemen and taken to a "madhouse," where the professor diagnoses him with "religious paranoia" for taking Christ's imitation literally.
    *   Reflects on the "growing plant" sprouting new growth in an opposite direction without losing balance. His thinking is on the right, his feeling on the left, leading him to the "master who governs this other realm."
    *   He embraces chaos ("eternal disorder," "unending multiplicity" of figures) as a necessary gateway to breaking through the mundane, and the dead as parts of his own history and psychology that demand attention.
    *   He must "live his animal" to treat his brother correctly and redeem the "roaming dead." Hypocrisy and false idealism will only lead to further suffering.
    *   He emphasizes the importance of silence, solitude, and dedicating the night to "bringing about the salvation of the dead."
*   **Cap. XVI: Nox tertia [Third Night]:**
    *   His soul whispers to him: "Have you recognized your madness, and do you admit it?... Let the light of your madness shine." Madness is a "special form of the spirit" and life itself is illogical.
    *   He enters a madhouse on a ship, confronting "Nietzsche" (his neighbor), who claims to be "Christ, the Savior" but is held captive by the "devil" (the professor) who "gets [the Mother of God] with child" each night, only for the child to be killed.
    *   Jung interprets this as pure mythology, recognizing the symbolic nature of the experience. He sees a "cross from which a serpent hangs," a "sun of martyrdom" pouring "bloody rays."
    *   He acknowledges the "hellish webs of words," where "emptiness and fullness flow together," and the "word is an image of God."
    *   He realizes that "chaos must come over men," that the "anointed of this time" is a spirit "not in flesh," and that "what is done to this God you do to the lowest in yourself, under the law of love according to which nothing is cast out."
    *   The "lowest in you is the source of mercy," requiring him to take on "baseness and contemptibility" for God to be "healed."
    *   He accepts "bloodshed" for the renewed God, like Christ accepting sacrifice, proclaiming that he will break himself apart to unite opposites.
    *   He embraces his "lowest," seeing the "tree of life" with roots in Hell and top in Heaven, learning that "unlearning all distinctions save that concerning direction is part of your salvation."
    *   This leads to "steadfastness in great doubt"—the "veritable flower of life."
*   **Cap. XVII: Nox quarta [Fourth Night]:**
    *   His soul urges breaking "the hinges" between opposites (here/there, yes/no, above/below, left/right) to allow free passage and flow, acknowledging the "eternal confusion" and cosmic balance.
    *   He encounters the "fat cook" (from Chapter XV, now the librarian’s cook) and debates the "religious madness" of *The Imitation of Christ* verses the superficiality of modern science.
    *   He returns to the library, pushing aside "green curtains" to reveal a theater-like scene: Klingsor’s magic garden, with Amfortas and Kundry (now the librarian and his cook). Parsifal (Jung himself) enters, wearing a lionskin, wielding a club, but also modern trousers.
    *   Parsifal catches Klingsor's feather, signaling a shift. Jung removes his "armor layered with history" and "chimerical decoration," washes his feet in a spring, and puts on "civilian clothes," becoming "one with myself" (the kneeling audience).
    *   This is an acceptance of his "other" that makes him "completely human."
    *   He laments the "sadness" and "emptiness" of human pride and ambition, realizing the "suffering of the creative" who carry "something evil in them."
    *   He learns that "the warmest life contains good and evil inseparably and indistinguishably"; this is not the goal but the "way and the crossing," the "mother of all abominable deeds and all salutary symbols."
    *   His "filthy human heart" must bear the "strangler," the "terrible worm of God," which is the "son," to be born from "filth and power in raving blindness."
    *   He struggles with the serpent/bird transformation and the "unsolvable question of love," recognizing that "love never ends" is the "mystery of the crown and the serpent." He is "hanged between sky and earth," on the tree of life, accepting this state.
    *   Satan attempts to tempt him to "recant" his "reconciliation of opposites," but Jung refuses, embracing weakness as a "way."
    *   He learns from the bird (transformed serpent/soul): "If you love the earth, you are hanged; if you love the sky, you hover." He learns balance.
    *   The final imagery is of the "completion of the third day" (creation), marking a new phase.
    *   He reflects on his "tower" built from the underworld and upperworld, an enduring structure where the "devil is forged into the foundations." He has become "master of my own self."
    *   He encounters a "poisoner" from Hell, who committed murder "to honor God," leading to reflections on the "impersonality" of the beyond.
    *   He takes on the "nature of the serpent" to deny the devil influence. He has drawn "darkness from [his] beyond over into the day," satisfying the demands of the dead.
    *   He confronts his soul, who asserts that "nothing is accomplished yet" and "life has yet to begin" on a higher level.
    *   His soul (as Salome) is now "seeing," and Elijah gives her to him as a "gift." Jung, though married, accepts her (as pleasure, not love), as his own painful "ordeal" led to her healing.
    *   He becomes "crucified" by "unrelenting necessity" but ultimately embraces the "new freedom" of being bound to neither men nor (external) Gods, but to his own path and his love for his soul.

**SCRUTINIES**

*   **Scrutinies: {1} (Jung confronts his "I")**
    *   Jung resists his "hollow nothing" self ("I"), recognizing its "main flaw": a lack of "self-esteem" masked by boastfulness.
    *   He subjects this "I" to psychological "torture" (analogous to medieval inquisition "in majorem Dei gloriam"), forcing it to acknowledge its "laughably sensitive, self-righteous, unruly, mistrustful, pessimistic, cowardly, dishonest, venomous, vengeful" nature.
    *   He aims to "whip" his "I" to endure the "wildness of others," to overcome its sensitivity and self-pity, and to accept inferiority.
    *   He challenges its "hypocritical composure," "shameless" hidden actions, and self-serving "love," demanding it speak of its shame rather than grandiosity.
    *   He declares, "The dear old God has died," replaced by a "fiery worm" (the new God) that "lets fire rain on the earth." This new God demands "self-torment" for the forgiveness of sins, revealing the "I"'s "childish pride" and "bad, loving God" who excuses its sinfulness.
    *   He vows to "burn out" the "I"'s pride, make it "empty," and humble it to accept its "ugliness and ridiculousness."
*   **Scrutinies: {2} (Dialogue with the Soul)**
    *   After self-lashing, Jung can bear solitude. His soul speaks from a distance, "above [him]," having become "sunlike."
    *   Jung is in the "dark smoke" of earthly life, but his soul's voice sounds closer. She urges him to create.
    *   He struggles with his "I," which feels burdened by the "dead." His soul affirms his "bitterness," stating: "It is not your severity, it is not your cruelty, but necessity. The way of life is sown with fallen ones."
    *   His soul, ascending after the God, tells him his disappearance from his human world was "inner necessity" and that he has "enjoyed utmost benefit."
    *   He reflects on the inadequacy of "mere belief" in modern times, emphasizing "differentiating knowledge" to understand the "confusion" caused by the soul's discovery.
    *   He fears the "madness that befalls the solitary" but embraces his path despite it.
    *   An old man (a "nameless one" from the desert) appears, urging him to "practice solitude assiduously" and to "take your leave from science," which is "too superficial."
    *   He hears a voice (Philemon) saying "The worst comes last" and riddling sentences ("Nonsense streams from the deepest wells..."). The outbreak of WWI confirms that his internal conflict was a collective one.
*   **Scrutinies: {3} (Philemon's Teaching on Gold and Self-Forgetfulness)**
    *   Jung sees an osprey seizing a fish and rising upward, interpreting it as a sign of "what is below is borne upward."
    *   Philemon (now identified explicitly) appears, seeking to "turn [Jung] around" and "master" him, to "emboss [him] like a coin," making him "the will of the whole."
    *   Philemon's "ambiguous speech" about gold as an "inexorable ruler" that "sleeps and shines" symbolizes the inherent value of the self, which men seek but few find.
    *   He teaches that giving is "deceitful" if not earned; true value comes from acquisition, not unearned gifts.
    *   Philemon falls silent, leaving Jung to recognize that "high barriers" must be erected between men, not for protection, but against "mutual virtues" that lead to "mutual enchantment."
    *   "Self-forgetting virtue" is "unnatural alienation" from one's essence. True redemption means accepting oneself, not becoming a slave to others' expectations.
    *   He concludes that "to give all one's goods to the poor means to educate them to become idle." Pity should be a "strict educator."
    *   He emphasizes the necessity of "constantly going to oneself to re-establish the connection with the self" to reach God. But also fears that the "God appears to us in a certain state of the soul" and "makes us sick."
    *   He must "free [his] self from the God" to live, accepting that the God is more than just good.
*   **Scrutinies: {4} (HAP and the Dead's Demand)**
    *   Three "shades" (the dead) approach, led by a woman (his former patient who recovered Egyptian mysteries). She needs a "symbol, the mediator," and calls for "light."
    *   Jung is given a "sign": HAP, a phallic, "terribly simple, initially stupid, naturally godlike" symbol, which is "God's other pole," the "flesh spirit, the blood spirit," the "extract of all bodily juices."
    *   Horrified, Jung sees this as "abysmal evil," but the shade urges him to "drink blood" and "suck" from the "carcass" (life juices of men) to gain knowledge and share their life.
    *   She reveals that HAP is the "summit of the church that still lies sunken," and that the "God of the spirit is in the night." The "pregnant" Heavenly Brimo gave birth to the dragon, then HAP, who is "the rebellion of the Below."
    *   Jung is exhausted, realizing the dead want his life ("blood") and for him to live with "shadows." He is to provide the "warmth of life" that unites good and evil. He must sacrifice his "personal devil" (superfluous pain) for "true joy" of fulfillment.
    *   He refuses to become a prophet or build a church, but his soul insists: "I want the church, it is necessary for you and for others" because "community with the dead" is needed.
    *   He must offer his "blood" and "body" (his life and desire) as a "Last Supper" for the dead to gain "speech and life." This is to save both him and them from "inextinguishable and unrelenting power of vivid longing."
*   **Scrutinies: {5} (Soul's Ascent and Philemon's Challenge)**
    *   His soul ascends to the "upper realms," irradiated by the Godhead's brilliance. Jung feels "crucified on the tree of life," "unmanly torment," his virtues "torn apart" to make his "human animal" palatable.
    *   He challenges his soul's "betrayal:" why does she seek to draw his life into the "glaring white light of the Godhead," leaving him with the "mud" of humanity?
    *   Philemon appears and reveals that he gave Jung these words, advising him to "remove, Oh man, the divine, too, from your soul," for the soul is a "tyrant" when it wields divine power.
    *   Philemon warns against the soul's "whoring" and "maliciousness" when neglected, likening it to a "hellish-divine treasure" to be guarded.
    *   Jung must break the "bloody veil" woven by the "lost soul," capture the "divine whore," and force her into "confinement" where she can find "resistance and her own law." He must make his "human hammer" (humanity) rule over the Gods and daimons.
    *   His soul, though saddened, prepares to leave, hinting at a hidden "golden vessel" (love). Jung accuses her of "lying" and "stealing" from men, until she reveals that the stolen treasure is "love, warm human love, blood, warm red blood, the holy source of life."
    *   Jung asserts that "love belongs to me" and that he will be its administrator, protecting the "holy source" from the Gods' insatiable greed. He demands that the "Gods" and "daimons" learn humility from men.
    *   Philemon concludes that "Both God and man are disappointed victims of deception," praising "endless multiplicity" and "diversity" as the true wealth.
*   **Scrutinies: {6} (The Three Prophecies: War, Magic, Religion)**
    *   Jung's soul, after hesitating, begins to speak again. Jung demands she honor "humankind" and bring about "earthly fortune" for men, not just for her own "salvation." He offers his "silent" support for her work.
    *   She commands him to "build the furnace," throwing in the "old, the broken, the worn out" for renewal, shaping matter to achieve "salvation." Matter is the "filling of emptiness," "stronger than HAP," the "hardest matter is the best."
    *   His soul then has an anxious vision of "blazing fire" and "an immeasurable sea of embers"—a "searing miracle." Jung is terrified of this destruction.
    *   The soul insists this is the "ruler of this world" demanding the "sacrifice of your fear," to bring "mercy." Jung fears this is a betrayal.
    *   His soul tells him he "possess[es] the word that should not be allowed to remain concealed," despite his sense of being a "stammering minor."
    *   She reveals a vision of a "sea of fire" consuming the world, with Jung (a "crazy look in his eyes") going "before the fire in a burning robe," announcing its agony, bearing its mark. Men won't believe him but will see his "mark."
    *   Jung despairs, crying, "Which fire?"
*   **Scrutinies: {7} (Septem Sermones ad Mortuos - First to Third Sermons)**
    *   A "dark crowd" (the dead) knocks, and his soul appears, saying, "They are here and will tear open your door." Jung resists, but realizes he needs to listen.
    *   The dead, returning from Jerusalem unable to find what they sought, demand "light" (the "symbol, the mediator").
    *   Philemon teaches them: The **Pleroma** is "nothingness or fullness," infinite, eternal, lacking qualities yet possessing all. **Creation** is its differentiation, and our essence is differentiation (principium individuationis). Not differentiating leads to "dissolution in nothingness." Qualities come in **pairs of opposites** (effective/ineffective, light/dark, good/evil), which cancel out in the Pleroma but are effective in us, making us "victims." True seeking is for one's "own essence."
    *   The dead grumble ("You are a pagan, a polytheist!") because Philemon "takes from men the Gods to whom they could pray."
    *   Philemon asserts he teaches the dead a God they *know*, not believe in, a God unknown to "living men" whose age requires knowing, not merely believing. He teaches "Abraxas."
    *   **Abraxas** is the "God who is difficult to grasp," combining the **Summum Bonum** (from the sun) and **Infinum Malum** (from the devil). Abraxas is "LIFE, altogether indefinite, the mother of good and evil." He is force, duration, change, and "improbable probability." He generates truth/lying, good/evil in the same act, making him "terrible" but "redemptive" if not "resisted." God (Helios) is "behind the sun," the devil "behind the night."
    *   Philemon's teachings outrage the dead again (Christians) but he argues they had rejected the loving God for one in whom "everything created is nothing" (because he is creator and destroyer). He teaches them the "God who dissolves unity" because love failed to unite them; "Fear compels" what love does not.
*   **Scrutinies: {8} (Septem Sermones ad Mortuos - Fourth Sermon)**
    *   The dead demand more about the "highest God." Philemon speaks of **Eros** (the Burning One, flame, consumes) and the **Tree of Life** (the Growing One, greens, heaps up matter).
    *   "Good and evil unite" in both. Life and love oppose in their divinity.
    *   The Gods are "innumerable" like stars (each star a God, space a devil); **Abraxas** is the "effect of the whole."
    *   Four principal Gods: Sun God (beginning), Eros (binds two), Tree of Life (fills space), Devil (opens what is closed, destroys).
    *   Philemon condemns replacing "multiplicity" with a "single God," which "mutilates creation" and "maims" human nature.
    *   Equality is for *man*, not God. Gods live in solitude, separated by "vast distances," needing **communion** to "bear their separateness."
    *   He clarifies the "dark Gods" (earthly, diminish) vs. "bright Gods" (heavenly, magnify).
    *   The dead laugh and mock his "foolishness," but Philemon insists his teaching frees them from their "mockery" and "belief" by adding "folly to your cleverness, this unreason to your reason." This new wisdom will allow them to "live onward to the eternal light."
    *   The dead, though initially outraged, slowly creep away silently.
*   **Scrutinies: {9} (Septem Sermones ad Mortuos - Fifth and Sixth Sermons)**
    *   The dead demand instruction on "church and holy communion." Philemon explicates **Spirituality** (celestial mother, conceives and embraces, womanlike, heavenly in man, earthly in woman) and **Sexuality** (Phallos, engenders and creates, manlike, earthly in man, spiritual in woman).
    *   If men and women do not differentiate their spiritual and sexual ways, they become "devils to each other." These are "superhuman daimons" (Mother and Phallos) that reveal the "world of the Gods."
    *   **Community** is necessary due to human weakness against Gods/daimons. In community, one submits to others; in **singleness**, one places oneself above others to avoid slavery. "Abstention" in community, "extravagance" in singleness. Community gives "warmth," singleness "light."
    *   He describes the daimon of sexuality as a "serpent" (half human soul, thought-desire, earthly, mischievous, whore) and the daimon of spirituality as a "white bird" (half celestial soul, desire-thought, chaste, solitary, messenger).
    *   The dead are contemptuous ("We have known this for a long time"), but Philemon smiles, saying they are "poor souls" clinging to "mockery" of belief. His wisdom frees them from "greed for men."
    *   His "knowledge is the axe of the sacrificer" that "fanned a glowing fire," "rent open healed-over wounds," leading to the earth becoming "green and fruitful again from the blood of the sacrifice."
*   **Scrutinies: {10} (Septem Sermones ad Mortuos - Seventh Sermon)**
    *   Philemon reveals the final mystery: "Man is a gateway," through which one passes from the outer world to the inner, smaller infinity.
    *   "At immeasurable distance a lonely star stands in the zenith." This is "man's one God," his world, his Pleroma, his divinity. "In this world, man is Abraxas," creator and destroyer of his own world.
    *   Prayer to this star "increases the light," throws a bridge over death, eases great longing. "Nothing stands between man and his one God" if he turns his eyes from Abraxas.
    *   The dead, accepting this, ascend "like smoke."
    *   Philemon confirms that man is a "gateway" through which the Gods pass, and that "being is eternal in each moment." He saved "being from time."
    *   He says the dark and golden treasure with blue starlight will be given "When you have surrendered everything that wants to burn to the holy flame."
*   **Scrutinies: {11} (Encounter with Death and the Star-Nature)**
    *   A "dark form with golden eyes" approaches (Death), bringing "abstinence—abstinence from human joy and suffering." He is a "stranger" who brings "quiet pain and long peace," veiling man to allow his "stellar nature" to begin.
    *   Philemon opens Jung's gaze to the "immeasurable mystery": the night sky in the form of a woman with a sevenfold starry mantle (the World Mother).
    *   He declares, "Mother, you who stand in the higher circle... he wants to become your child." A voice from afar demands Jung "cleanse himself first" of "human suffering and joy," "commingling with men."
*   **Scrutinies: {12} (Dismembering and Love's Fidelity)**
    *   A wise man (Philemon) appears, offering "healing fire" and "love of women," which Jung fears as "dismembering" and "dissolution."
    *   Philemon urges him to be "blown apart and shredded," to embrace the "Last Supper" of self-sacrifice. His uniqueness will be "stolen from himself."
    *   He realizes he must "remain true to love" to resolve the "commingling" of unlived love, achieving release from bondage by voluntary devotion and suffering "dismembering," reaching "bonding with the great mother."
    *   He must overcome "sin" and self-deceit to be true to love.
    *   Philemon appears with a silver fish, explaining that Christ ("master and brother") made "animals" into "men," but men continue their "craving" and "demand" from Him.
    *   Philemon (speaking as Christ) declares his work "completed," that "mankind has grown older and a new month has begun." Each man must now do his "own work of redemption."
    *   Jung accepts this.
*   **Scrutinies: {13} (The Serpent of the God and the New Light)**
    *   Philemon describes the serpent of the God, born from the "impregnated dead body of the underworld," demanding human blood as sacrifice to grow strong. He equates living with "deceiving the God."
    *   Elijah and Salome reappear, expressing confusion about the "new" world and the "death of God."
    *   Elijah hears a "thunderous pagan roar"—the voices of the wild. Jung explains that the "one God is dead," disintegrating into "many Gods" and "daimons," making the world "rich overnight."
    *   The soul "divided herself" into bird and serpent, then father and mother, then Elijah and Salome. He separates Elijah and Salome from his soul, placing them among the "daimons," as they represent the past and future, not the present "being of men."
    *   Elijah is saddened by the "multiplicity"; Salome embraces it ("the many is more pleasurable").
    *   Jung asserts his "God" is the "multiplicity of individual things" (one multiple God) and the "uniqueness of the one thing" (another God).
    *   His soul, now worried, feels "torment of the depths" and the "suffering of the Gods." She tells him that "the Gods need a human mediator and rescuer."
    *   He acknowledges the Gods' torment but demands "payment in kind," refusing unconditional obedience. The Gods are "insatiable" from too many sacrifices; "dearth makes contentment."
    *   His soul, as a bird and a serpent, consults the Gods and returns to say they "give in." The "devil is helpful" in breaking compulsion, but he must seek counsel from the Gods.
    *   He cleanses himself from "commingling" and accepts the "pain of one-sidedness."
*   **Scrutinies: {14} (Philemon's Teaching on Shame and Love)**
    *   Jung finds Philemon in his garden. Philemon is addressed by a "blue shade" (Christ or the master) as a king. Philemon reveals he and Baucis (Helena) are hosts to this "terrible worm" (the shade/Christ principle), and thus the shade comes to him.
    *   Philemon confirms that the shade's nature is "also of the serpent," recognizing his suffering and descent into Hell before ascent.
    *   The shade brings "the beauty of suffering."
    *   Philemon reveals the soul as a "devilish farce," a "tyrant," a "bloodthirsty daimon" when it arrogates divine power. He urges Jung to "lock her up," to make "man rule in the human world."
    *   Jung's soul, now returned, sadly accepts confinement but claims "love, warm human love" as her sacred stolen ornament.
    *   Jung asserts "love belongs to me" and that he will be its administrator, protecting it from the Gods' "greed for greed" and "insatiableness."
    *   Philemon concludes that "Both God and man are disappointed victims of deception," and that "endless multiplicity" replaces forced unity.
    *   Jung's soul hesitates to leave, concealing a "golden vessel" (love). Jung (as "hound") demands she "learn to honor humankind," for "I saw the land that is promised to man, the land where milk and honey flows."
    *   He tells her to build the "furnace" of renewal, shaping "hardest matter" with care.
*   **Scrutinies: {15} (The Fire of the Gods and the Cosmic Embrace)**
    *   His soul foresees a "blazing fire" that "sets the towns and villages on fire," with Jung running "before the fire in a burning robe," announcing its agony.
    *   A "dark crowd" wants to "tear open [his] door." His soul (as Philemon) helps him to face them, telling them to reveal who they are.
    *   The dead return, having "not found what we sought" in Jerusalem; they seek his "light," not his blood.
    *   Philemon (now as the one delivering the Sermons) explains that the dead "rejected the single and highest God," needing to learn about the "multifarious and extended" Gods whom they know unconsciously.
    *   He argues that man's history (weighing, numbering, apportioning things) has led to things now "speak[ing]" and "devouring millions of men." Many Gods "powerfully raise their voices."
    *   Philemon (as the "father") insists on the importance of "multiplicity," arguing that "the Gods are many, while men are few."
    *   He then teaches the final three sermons on **Spirituality** (Mater Coelestis, womanlike, conceives/embraces) and **Sexuality** (Phallos, manlike, engenders/creates). He warns against confusing them and against their daimonic nature.
    *   He concludes the *Septem Sermones* with a vision of **Man as a gateway** to an inner world where "a lonely star" (man's one God) stands in the zenith. "In this world, man is Abraxas," creator and destroyer of his own world.
    *   Philemon then reveals a "dark form with golden eyes" (Death) that brings "abstinence" and "healing," transforming Jung's "solar nature" into "stellar nature."
    *   Philemon shows him the night sky as a woman (Mother, nameless and seven-veiled) who must accept Jung's "birth," leading to a vision where Jung knows he must leave men and be "secluded."
    *   A wise doctor (Philemon) then tempts him with "joy," "healing fire," and "love of women," which Jung fears as "dismembering."
    *   Philemon reveals that his "uniqueness" will be "stolen from himself" by becoming a "river that pours forth over the lands," connecting Earth and Heaven. This process demands fidelity, suffering, and voluntary devotion to love to dissolve cosmic "commingling."
    *   He hears Philemon's final words about the serpent of God and the need for human sacrifice to feed it, leading to a new "first appearing light."
    *   Elijah and Salome reappear, expressing bewilderment at the "death of God" and the "new garb" of the world. Elijah hears a "thunderous pagan roar"—the voices of ancestors and the wild.
    *   Jung explains the "one God is dead" and has "disintegrated into the many," making the world rich and the soul "multiple." He separates Elijah and Salome from his soul, placing them among the "daimons."
    *   The Gods (now many) are outraged by Jung's lack of "obedience," but he resolves to maintain his "dignity before the Gods," demanding they "devise a service in return."
    *   His soul (as a bird/serpent) confirms the Gods "give in" and that the "devil is helpful" in breaking the law.
    *   Philemon encounters a "blue shade" (Christ as king), whom he hosts. Philemon acknowledges that Christ's "nature is also of the serpent," and that he brings "the beauty of suffering."
    *   Jung's dialogue with his soul ends with her returning to the divine realm, but he has learned that his path involves continuing to make of himself a "furnace" of transformation.

Epilogue (1959):
*   Jung reflects on having worked on *The Red Book* for 16 years, stating his "acquaintance with alchemy in 1930" and the text *The Secret of the Golden Flower* in 1928 provided the means to "absorb the overpowering force of the original experiences" and "arrange them into a whole."
*   He admits that to a "superficial observer" the book would appear as "madness," but alchemy helped him to process it, preventing him from succumbing to it.
*   He emphasizes the "precious" nature of these experiences, justifying the costliness of the book, and his commitment to documenting the images that emerged, even though he felt the undertaking was "frightfully inadequate." The text trails off midsentence, symbolizing the ongoing, unfinished nature of his internal journey.

**Appendix B: Commentaries**
*   This appendix provides further interpretive background, specifically a commentary on chapters 9, 10, and 11 of *Liber Primus*.
*   **"Mysterium. Encounter" (pp. 86-89):** Interprets Elijah as Logos (spiritual principle, understanding, foresight, wisdom) and Salome as Eros (unspiritual principle of feeling, desire, longing, pleasure). The serpent signifies "everything dangerous and everything bad" in Logos and Eros as dark, unconscious principles. Salome's blindness indicates Eros's "incompleteness" and "subordination of Eros to Logos." The appearance of Eve vs. Mother of God with child represents carnal vs. spiritual Eros, with the former leading astray, the latter turning desire toward spirit, but potentially becoming "secular power-drive."
*   The commentary highlights the tension between the Ego's desire for consciousness and the need to embrace the undeveloped and repressed aspects of the soul (Eros), even if it means "sin" or "descent to a deeper level."
*   **"Instruction" (pp. 103-119):** Analyzes the movement of Salome (Eros) "toward the left" (inauspicious, un-conscious will), requiring guidance from Logos. The imagery of the mother and child leading to Peter and the Pope symbolizes a movement toward "spiritual power" that can become self-serving if not balanced. The Buddha sitting in fire represents transcending pleasure and suffering, becoming "unaffected and untouchable." The Kali figure represents suppressed passion becoming destructive.
*   **"Resolution" (pp. 127-150):** Interprets the two serpents as good and evil, with the black serpent turning white signifying the ineffectiveness of this opposition within the self. The ascent to the "temple of the sun" and encountering Mime represents the acquisition of wisdom from the depths. The serpent around the cross symbolizes the unification of opposites ("good and evil has become one"). The Izdubar experience signifies the necessary confrontation and integration of the blind hero (excessive will, power) with "seeing limitation and the incapacity of cleverness."

*   This entry provides a "preliminary sketch of [the] cosmology of the *Septem Sermones*."
*   Jung outlines three "ages": the current age of the "Frog God" (son's rulership), the age of allocation and harmony, followed by a third.
*   He describes his individual psychological structure: "Below you serpent, within you man, and above you God." The serpent symbolizes "poison and shrewdness," the dove (celestial soul) "love and foresight."
*   He elaborates on the division of the "conjoined" self into serpent, human soul, and celestial soul.

*   He emphasizes the solitary, starlike "one God" within, beyond Abraxas, as the "wonderfully beautiful and kind."

He must live his life fully to free himself from Abraxas's power, embracing pain and disappointment to find the "blue light of the star God." His soul is his "intercessor with Abraxas," protecting him and channeling human experience to the "one God."

Crucial Case Studies/Examples:

1.  **Jung's Precognitive Visions of War (1913-1914):** His visions of a destructive flood and a blood-red sea are central. Initially, he feared them as signs of his own impending madness. However, when WWI broke out, he interpreted them as collective events, validating his inner experiences as not merely personal delusions but profound insights into the collective unconscious and the fate of humanity. This realization was a turning point, dispelling his fear of psychosis and providing external confirmation for his inner world.
2.  **The Murder of Siegfried:** Jung's dream of murdering Siegfried, the German hero, is a crucial symbolic act. It represents the necessary killing of an outdated ideal within himself—his "hero ideal of efficiency"—and the prevailing German cultural archetype. This "assassination" of the old God/hero is depicted as a painful but vital step for personal and collective renewal, paving the way for a new psychological attitude and a "new God."
3.  **The Encounter with Izdubar (Gilgamesh):** The wounded, paralyzed giant Izdubar, symbolizing ancient heroic power blinded by modern scientific knowledge ("poison"), represents Jung's wrestling with the legacy of pre-rational, mythic consciousness. His act of carrying Izdubar as an "egg" (a "living fantasy") signifies the work of integrating the discarded, mythical essence back into consciousness, making it portable and allowing it to heal and transform. This is a profound re-evaluation of how mythological figures are understood and assimilated.
4.  **Elijah and Salome:** These archetypal figures, personifying Logos (spirit, rationality, foresight) and Eros (feeling, desire, temptation, the blind feminine), are central to Jung's internal dialogues. Their paradoxical and often contradictory relationship (Elijah as prophet-father, Salome as his blind, tempting, yet sacred daughter, sometimes identified as Jung's soul) forces Jung to grapple with the deepest tensions between spirit and flesh, order and chaos, and reveals the need to integrate these opposites rather than repress them. Their ongoing interplay is a primary means for Jung to understand the dynamics of the unconscious.
5.  **The Septem Sermones ad Mortuos (Seven Sermons to the Dead):** This Gnostic-style cosmology, delivered by Philemon to the "dead," provides a theoretical framework for Jung's experiences. It introduces the Pleroma (undifferentiated primal reality), Abraxas (the paradoxical, encompassing God of life), and the essential role of **differentiation** (the *principium individuationis*) in creation. This is not just a personal fantasy but a comprehensive myth-making act that clarifies the nature of God, Satan, and humanity, asserting that man is a "gateway" to the inner world and that a new psychological understanding is crucial for the dead's (and humanity's) salvation.

Critical Reception/Counter-arguments:

Upon its publication, *The Red Book* was met with immense academic and public interest, often described as a pivotal, once-in-a-lifetime release that fundamentally alters our understanding of Jung.

*   **Positive Reception:** Researchers and Jungian scholars hailed it as the missing centerpiece of Jung's *oeuvre*, a direct window into the genesis of his psychological concepts. It provided undeniable evidence of the experiential, subjective roots of his theories, challenging perceptions of him as a purely academic or dispassionate theorist. Its artistic merit, particularly Jung's calligraphy and paintings, was widely admired. It has been seen as a profound document of spiritual crisis and renewal relevant to contemporary searches for meaning.
*   **Challenges/Potential Criticisms:**
    *   **The "Madness" Question:** Jung himself agonized over whether the work would be perceived as madness. While scholars now largely view it as a controlled self-experiment rather than psychosis, its highly idiosyncratic, visionary, and often bizarre content might still challenge readers accustomed to conventional intellectual discourse.
    *   **Subjectivity vs. Objectivity:** Some critics might question the scientific generalizability of a work so deeply rooted in one individual's subjective experiences. However, Jung's later work aimed to demonstrate the universality of these patterns through comparative mythology and patient case studies.
    *   **Historical and Cultural Context:** Its deep immersion in turn-of-the-century European intellectual and spiritual currents, Gnosticism, and various mythologies might make it challenging for some modern readers without extensive background knowledge, despite the comprehensive introduction and notes.
    *   **Lack of Direct Scholarly Argumentation:** Jung deliberately kept scholarly references to a minimum in the *Red Book* itself, making it more akin to a sacred text or a work of art than a conventional academic treatise. This stylistic choice, while integral to its purpose, might be seen as difficult for those seeking a more straightforward presentation of theory.
    *   **Ambiguity and Paradox:** The book revels in ambiguity and paradox, which can be unsettling for those seeking clear, definitive answers. Jung intentionally cultivates this, but it requires a specific kind of engagement that some readers may resist.

3.  "Disorder and meaninglessness are the mother of order and meaning. Order and meaning are things that have become and are no longer becoming. If you marry the ordered to the chaos you produce the divine child, the supreme meaning beyond meaning and meaninglessness." (Liber Primus, Chapter III)
4.  "Everything that becomes too old becomes evil, the same is true of your highest. Learn from the suffering of the crucified God that one can also betray and crucify a God, namely the God of the old year. If a God ceases being the way of life, he must fall secretly." (Liber Primus, Chapter VI)
5.  "Abraxas is the God who is difficult to grasp. His power is greatest, because man does not see it. From the sun he draws the *summum bonum*; from the devil the *infinum malum*; but from Abraxas *LIFE*, altogether indefinite, the mother of good and evil." (Scrutinies, {7}, Second Sermon to the Dead)

Mental Models:

The author explicitly encourages the adoption of several mental models:

1.  **The Dichotomy of "Spirit of This Time" vs. "Spirit of the Depths":** This model allows one to critically assess prevailing societal values (rationality, utility, scientific materialism) against the deeper, often irrational, myth-making forces of the human psyche. It encourages questioning surface-level truths and seeking meaning in profound, often uncomfortable, inner experiences.
2.  **The Psyche as a Living Entity with Autonomous Figures (Active Imagination):** Instead of viewing unconscious contents as mere symptoms or reducible elements, this model treats them as active, communicating 'beings' (anima, shadow, wise old man, etc.) with their own intent and wisdom. This shifts one from a passive observer to an active participant in an inner drama.
3.  **The Individuation Process as Universal Development:** This framework posits life's ultimate goal not as external achievement or conformity, but as the unfolding of one's unique totality (the Self) through conscious integration of all psychic aspects, including the "lowest" and "evil." It's a lifelong journey of becoming who one genuinely is, often involving a "midlife transition" and "revaluation of earlier values."
4.  **Symbolic Thinking:** Moving beyond purely literal or allegorical interpretation, this model views symbols as dynamic expressions of paradoxical truths that unify conscious and unconscious contents. It acknowledges their inherent ambiguity and "meaning beyond meaning." "If the word is a sign, it means nothing. But if the word is a symbol, it means everything."
5.  **Embracing Paradox and Ambiguity:** Instead of seeking unequivocal answers or resolving opposites cleanly, this model accepts that fundamental truths often exist in tension and reconciliation (e.g., sense/nonsense, good/evil, life/death, Logos/Eros). "Ambiguity is the way of life."
6.  **The God-Image as Internal & Evolving:** Understanding that "God is an image" within the psyche, not just an external metaphysical entity. This image is dynamic, can "sicken," "die," and be "reborn" through human consciousness and sacrifice, reflecting the psychological state of the individual and the collective.
7.  **The Necessity of Confronting and Integrating One's "Shadow":** Recognizing that "evil" is not just external but resides within, often "precisely right behind your virtues." This model compels self-confrontation, acknowledging one's "lowest," "baseness," and "contemptibility" as essential for wholeness and healing.

The 'How-To' Framework:

The book, though not a prescriptive self-help guide, presents a clear methodology for engaging with the unconscious:

1.  **Acknowledge a Crisis of Meaning/Soul-Loss:** The process begins with a profound sense of inner void, unfulfillment despite external success, or the inadequacy of prevailing societal/intellectual frameworks. Acknowledge the "spirit of the depths" is at work.
2.  **Turn Inward (Introversion of Libido):** Redirect desire and attention from external pursuits, people, and conventional thoughts towards internal experiences. Accept "solitude" and the inner "desert" as necessary conditions.
3.  **Practice Active Imagination:**
    *   **Deliberately Evoke Fantasies:** Intentionally relax critical attention and allow inner images, voices, and figures to emerge in a waking state. This means setting aside the ego's control.
    *   **Enter the Inner Drama:** Engage with these fantasies "as into a drama," interacting with inner figures as if they are real and autonomous beings. Do not merely observe but participate.
    *   **Record Accurately:** Document these experiences faithfully, without censoring or rationalizing, whether through writing, drawing, or painting ("write exactly what you see").
    *   **Engage in Dialogue:** Converse with the inner figures, asking questions, listening to their responses, even when they are disturbing or contradictory.
4.  **Embrace Paradox and Opposites:** Actively seek out and integrate seemingly contradictory elements (Logos/Eros, good/evil, light/dark, conscious/unconscious, human/animal). Recognize that meaning often arises from the "fusion of sense and nonsense" and the resolution of creative tension. "Marry the ordered to the chaos."
5.  **Confront the "Shadow" and "Lowest" Aspects:** Acknowledge and assimilate one's personal and collective "evil," "ugliness," and "incapacity." This includes recognizing one's complicity in collective suffering (e.g., war) and taking on the "bloody sacrificial flesh" of human nature. This is a process of "self-lashing" and rigorous self-accounting.
6.  **"Kill" Outdated Ideals and Gods:** Recognize that old ways of life, heroic ideals, and even existing God-images can become "sick" and must "fall" or be "sacrificed" from within oneself to allow for renewal and the emergence of new meaning. This is a deeply personal "Christification"—becoming Christ oneself, rather than imitating.
7.  **Cultivate Humility and "Simplemindedness" (from the Desert):** Realize the limits of intellectual cleverness and rational control. Be open to insights that defy conventional understanding, even if they seem "ridiculous and revolting." The "most profound lowliness" and "poverty of spirit" are gateways to deeper wisdom.
8.  **Nourish the Emerging God-Image/Self:** Foster the divine child in the soul, recognizing it as the inherent totality. This involves accepting its "childlike" or "childish" nature and becoming its "mother" figure, nurturing its growth.
9.  **Translate Inner Experience to Outer Life (Confrontation with the World):** Once inner integration has proceeded, find ways to express these inner truths in a manner that can inform and contribute to the external world, without necessarily becoming a "preaching prophet." This means living out the transformed attitude, recognizing one's role in the "life of the earth."
10. **Embrace the "Magic" of the Unknown:** Recognize that life is fundamentally mysterious and "incomprehensible." Embrace the "magical" as the working of unreason, which cannot be understood but can be engaged with through faith and openness.

Implementation Checklist:

1.  **Start a "Black Book" or Journal:** Dedicate a specific notebook to meticulously record all dreams, spontaneous fantasies, and inner dialogues *without interpretation or censoring*.
2.  **Practice Daily Active Imagination:** Set aside dedicated time (e.g., 15-30 minutes) to intentionally engage with an inner image, mood, or figure. Visualize it, ask it questions, and note down its responses and movements.
3.  **Draw or Paint Inner Experiences:** Regardless of artistic skill, visually render significant dreams, visions, or inner figures. Focus on conveying the experience rather than producing "art."
4.  **Identify Your "Spirit of This Time":** Reflect on the prevailing societal values, conventions, and personal ambitions that shape your conscious life. What do *you* prioritize in terms of "use and value"?
5.  **Seek Your "Spirit of the Depths":** Actively look for areas of personal discomfort, paradox, meaninglessness, or "madness" that challenge your conscious worldview. What has been suppressed?
6.  **Confront a "Shadow" Trait:** Choose one aspect of your personality you despise or deny, and consciously engage with it (e.g., through internal dialogue), seeking to understand its purpose or origin.
7.  **Identify an "Outdated God/Ideal":** Recognize a personal or collective ideal that no longer serves life, and symbolically "sacrifice" it or allow it to "fall" through introspection.
8.  **Reflect on Personal and Collective Guilt:** Consider areas where individual actions contribute to collective suffering, or how collective events are mirrored in personal struggles.
9.  **Embrace "Simplemindedness":** Allow for moments of not-knowing, curiosity, and even "folly," stepping away from intellectual hubris to observe without immediate judgment or explanation.
10. **Contemplate a Mandala:** Create or meditate on a circular image (mandala) to reflect on inner psychic order and wholeness, observing any spontaneous images or feelings that arise.

Before vs. After:

**Before:**

*   **Mindset:** Primarily rational, conscious, goal-oriented, valuing external success and societal approval. May feel a sense of spiritual alienation, inner emptiness, or unfulfillment despite outward achievements. Discomfort with irrationality, ambiguity, and emotions. May believe their existing (often implicitly Christian or scientific) worldview is absolute. Fear of madness or deviation.
*   **Behavior:** Driven by external laws, examples, and ambitions (the "spirit of this time"). Avoids solitary introspection or deep emotional "muddiness." Projects negative aspects onto others or dismisses them as "psychological problems." Might imitate external heroes or roles. Sees dreams as meaningless or reducible. Avoids confronting profound inner conflicts or "primitive" impulses.

**After:**

*   **Mindset:** Embraces paradoxical thinking, acknowledging the validity of both conscious rationality and unconscious irrationality. Values direct inner experience and symbolic understanding over superficial belief or dogma. Recognizes the dynamic, living nature of the psyche and its figures (anima, shadow, Self, Gods). Accepts a degree of personal "madness" and the inherent ambiguity of life as essential for wholeness. Cultivates humility and intellectual "poverty of spirit."
*   **Behavior:** Engages in active self-exploration, dialoguing with inner figures. Seeks to integrate "lowest" and "evil" aspects of the self, recognizing their transformative potential. Consciously cultivates a rich inner life, which then informs and enriches outer life. Lives a more self-determined, authentic life, no longer solely bound by external expectations or outdated ideals. Views both personal and collective events as manifestations of deeper psychic processes. Is a "servant of a child" (the emerging Self/new God), committed to the ongoing process of individuation rather than a static state. Recognizes the profound interconnection between individual inner work and the fate of the collective.
